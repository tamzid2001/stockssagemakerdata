Forecasting + Screening + Options: A Faster Quantura Workflow

Published February 17, 2026

Forecasts are only as useful as the decisions they unlock. The best teams connect their forecasts to a screening process, validate the market’s own expectations, and log each decision so the loop gets tighter every week. This post outlines a lightweight Quantura workflow that links forecasting, screening, and options analysis, plus small process upgrades that keep research consistent.

Open the terminal Run the screener

Workflow goals

Keep every step aligned to a decision-ready output.

  • Forecasts that translate into clear scenarios.
  • Screened lists that shrink the research surface area.
  • Options signals that validate price expectations.
  • A dashboard trail for follow-up and accountability.

1) Start with a forecast question

The fastest forecasts start with a clear question: “Where does uncertainty expand in the next 5 to 20 sessions?” In Quantura, load a ticker in the terminal and run a forecast band with your preferred engine. You are not hunting for a single price target. You are looking for a band that frames where risk shifts. Notice where the upper and lower quantiles diverge, and identify the levels that would invalidate the thesis. Those are your initial decision boundaries.

A good practice is to log the forecast horizon and the confidence width as a baseline. If the band is wide, treat the setup as exploratory. If the band is tight and directional, mark it for deeper validation.

Forecast checklist

Pick a horizon and document it
Compare trend vs. mean-reversion setups
Capture invalidation levels
Save the run for review

2) Use the screener to filter the universe

Forecasting should never start from the full market. Use the Quantura screener to narrow the list based on liquidity, volatility, and momentum. When the forecast band suggests volatility expansion, filter for names that already show rising ATR or widening ranges. If the band suggests compression, screen for stable, trending names that typically move in measured steps.

Once the screener returns a manageable list, push candidates back into the terminal. This keeps the workflow circular: the forecast informs the screen, and the screen feeds back into the forecast. You avoid wandering between tools and keep your hypothesis tied to the filters you actually ran.

Screener upgrades

Save a “volatility expansion” preset
Save a “trend confirmation” preset
Export candidates directly to the terminal
Document filters in your notes

3) Validate with the options chain

Options data gives you the market’s probabilistic lens. After identifying your forecast candidates, open the options chain to see where implied volatility is concentrated. Look for clusters of open interest or unusual volume around the levels you flagged in the forecast. If the options market prices a move that aligns with your band, you gain confidence. If the market prices a different direction or a wider move, treat that as a signal to reframe the forecast.

The goal is not to trade options by default. The goal is to compare your forecast with the market’s implied probabilities. This also helps you plan position sizing or alert thresholds. If implied volatility is elevated while your forecast band is narrow, you may be missing a catalyst. If implied volatility is low but your band is wide, your model might be overstating risk.

Options validation cues

Map IV skew to forecast direction
Note open interest near key levels
Compare IV percentile to history
Align alerts to implied ranges

4) Log the workflow in the dashboard

The dashboard is where the workflow becomes repeatable. Every time you run a forecast, capture the horizon, the band width, the screener filters, and the options validation note. This lets you review what worked, refine your presets, and spot where decisions drifted from the original plan. Over time, you can quantify which forecast questions lead to actionable trades or watchlist outcomes.

If you work with a team, this also gives you a shared language. Everyone can see the inputs, the forecast assumptions, and the implied probabilities. It prevents the “one chart screenshot” problem and keeps collaboration grounded in data.

Dashboard habits

Attach forecast runs to each ticker
Tag the screener preset used
Add options notes and IV levels
Schedule a follow-up review date

Putting it together: a 30-minute workflow

Here is a simple cadence that keeps research tight without cutting corners. Start in the terminal and run your forecast question. Move to the screener and narrow the universe to a handful of candidates. Validate those candidates with the options chain, then log the run in the dashboard. The output is a short list with a clear reason for each name and an agreed range of uncertainty. Repeat weekly, and the logs will show where your models are strongest.

  • Minutes 0-10: Forecast bands for two to three candidates.
  • Minutes 10-20: Screener refinement and candidate selection.
  • Minutes 20-30: Options validation and dashboard notes.
“The workflow matters more than any single model. Repeatability wins.”
— Quantura workflow note

Next steps

If you have not built presets yet, start with one screen for volatility expansion and one for trend confirmation. Then, build a short checklist for every forecast you save. The goal is to remove decision fatigue and focus attention on the handful of names that deserve it.

Ready to run the workflow? Launch the terminal, pull your screener preset, and pin the outcome in the dashboard.
